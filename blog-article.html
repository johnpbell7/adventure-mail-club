Why children who write letters
become better readers.

A look at the research — and what we see every month in replies.

Back to blog
AP
Alice Pemberton
Reading 8 min read · 14 March 2025
📖
When a child writes to someone who writes back — and uses their words — something changes in how they relate to language entirely.

The research on children's literacy has consistently shown that writing for a real audience — rather than completing exercises for a teacher — produces measurably better outcomes in both reading comprehension and creative output.

When a child knows their reply will be read, referenced, and built upon in the next letter they receive, the stakes of writing change entirely. The page is no longer a test to complete. It is a message to send.

What we see in the replies

In the first Adventure Mail Club letter, we see replies of five to ten words. By the third month, those same children are writing half a page. By the sixth, some are writing two pages — unprompted, unsolicited, just because they have more to say.

This is not because they have improved technically. It is because they care what happens next. And caring is the beginning of all real writing.

The consequence of being heard

One of the things we hear most often from parents is that their child came to show them a line from the letter — a line that used something from the child's own reply. "Mum, they mentioned the bridge. I told them about the bridge."

That moment — the moment of recognition — is when a child learns that their words have weight. That writing is not just transcription, but communication. That is where literacy becomes something a child wants, rather than something they do.

“She spent three days writing her reply. She kept revising it because she wanted it to be right. She said ‘I want them to be able to use it.’”
— Louise P., mum of Ellie, age 8 — Bristol
